When you create a campaign in [VTEX Ads](/en/docs/tracks/retail-media), you choose a **campaign strategy** that defines how the platform manages bids and prioritizes your ads to deliver a specific business outcome. The strategy you select determines the trade-off between visibility (reaching more users) and efficiency (extracting more return from each unit of ad spend).

This article describes each available strategy, when to use it, and how to set it in the campaign creation flow.

## Strategy overview

The strategies **Reach**, **Growth**, and **Profitability** work with predefined [ACOS](/en/docs/tracks/retail-media-glossary) ranges (the percentage of ad spend over the revenue generated by the ads). The fourth strategy, **Target ROAS**, lets you set a custom return-on-ad-spend goal and optimizes each ad individually to converge toward it.

| Strategy | Efficiency target | Best suited for |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Reach** | ACOS 30%-50% | New products or highly competitive categories |
| **Growth** | ACOS 15%-30% | Mid-rotation products |
| **Profitability** | ACOS 5%-15% | Best-selling products |
| **Target ROAS** | Advertiser-defined ROAS | Performance-driven campaigns with a specific revenue-to-spend goal |

For Reach, Growth, and Profitability, the platform automatically adjusts bids to keep ACOS within the strategy's suggested range. For Target ROAS, the platform converges to the specific value you enter.

> ℹ️ ACOS (`spend / revenue`, lower is better) and ROAS (`revenue / spend`, higher is better) describe the same relationship from opposite directions. A 20% ACOS is equivalent to a 5× ROAS. See the [Retail media glossary](/en/docs/tracks/retail-media-glossary) for definitions.

## Reach

Use **Reach** when the goal is to maximize the visibility of your products and put them in front of as many consumers as possible. The strategy tolerates a higher ACOS (30%-50%) so bids stay competitive enough to win auctions even on inventory with lower direct conversion.

Reach is best suited for:

- New products with limited sales history that need exposure to start gathering performance data.
- Categories with strong competition where higher bids are required to secure visibility.
- Awareness-focused campaigns where impressions and clicks matter more than immediate return.

## Growth

Use **Growth** when you want to balance volume and efficiency. The ACOS range (15%-30%) keeps bids competitive enough to scale sales while preserving a reasonable return on each unit of spend.

Growth is best suited for:

- Products with steady, mid-level sales velocity, where there is room to accelerate sales without compromising efficiency.
- Campaigns transitioning from a successful Reach phase into structured sales growth.
- Catalogs that include a mix of recurring sellers and emerging products.

## Profitability

Use **Profitability** when efficiency is the primary goal and you want to maximize return on each unit of spend. The lower ACOS range (5%-15%) keeps bids tightly controlled.

Profitability is best suited for:

- Best-selling products with strong organic conversion, where additional ad spend should produce a high direct return.
- Campaigns where the priority is ROI over reach.

> ⚠️ Tighter ACOS targets reduce bid competitiveness. With Profitability, ads may lose auctions more often, leading to a smaller share of impressions compared to less restrictive strategies.

## Target ROAS

Use **Target ROAS** when you want to set a specific return-on-ad-spend goal instead of a predefined ACOS range, and have the platform automatically manage bids to converge toward it.

Unlike Reach, Growth, and Profitability, which optimize at the campaign level, Target ROAS evaluates each ad × publisher pair independently. High-performing combinations gain priority and serve more often, while underperforming ones are progressively scaled back, all within the same campaign.

Target ROAS is best suited for:

- Performance-driven campaigns with a clear revenue-to-spend goal (for example, 3 units of revenue for every 1 unit invested).
- Multi-publisher (network) campaigns, where each publisher converts differently and uniform bids would either overpay weaker placements or underpay stronger ones.
- Mixed-performance catalogs that combine high-converting hero products with long-tail SKUs, where granular per-ad optimization avoids budget concentration on the wrong combinations.
- Reducing manual bid management while preserving a defined efficiency target.

### How Target ROAS works

The optimizer runs on a periodic schedule (approximately hourly) and, on every cycle, recalculates two things for each ad × publisher pair:

- A priority score (range `0.1`-`5.0`, baseline `1.0`) that modulates how often the pair wins auctions.
- The CPC or CPM bid itself, which converges progressively toward the value that would mathematically deliver the target ROAS given current conversion rates.

### Safeguards

Bids are always floored at each publisher's minimum CPC or CPM and capped at 25× the original bid to prevent runaway increases. For sponsored brand ads, both CPC and CPM are adjusted simultaneously.

The optimizer also includes the following safeguards:

- **Cold start (first 72 hours):** Pairs with no impressions or no spend are skipped. Their scores and bids are held at their last value until data accumulates.
- **Warm-up (first 7 days):** Adjustment intensity ramps up linearly to avoid acting on thin data. Pausing and resuming a campaign restarts the warm-up window.
- **Adaptive lookback window:** ROAS is calculated over 3, 7, or 14 days depending on each pair's impression volume in the trailing 7 days, so low-volume pairs are not over-reactive to noise.
- **Throttle:** When a pair's actual ROAS stays below 50% of the target for 3 or more consecutive cycles, an additional penalty progressively reduces its priority. The penalty reverses automatically as soon as the pair recovers above the threshold.

> ℹ️ Underperforming pairs are demoted, not paused. The campaign keeps running, but total delivery may slow if many pairs are throttled at the same time. Target ROAS optimizes bid efficiency, not budget pacing. Daily and lifetime budgets are consumed through normal delivery.

### Supported campaign formats

Target ROAS is available for the following [campaign types](/en/docs/tracks/retail-media#campaign-types):

- **CPC-based:** sponsored products and sponsored brands.
- **CPM-based:** banner, video, banner video, and sponsored brand video.

### Choosing your Target ROAS value

The value you enter directly drives bid behavior, so it should reflect the unit economics of the advertised products and the typical performance of similar campaigns in the same category. As a rule of thumb:

- **Realistic targets** allow the optimizer to converge and deliver predictably. The closer your target is to recent historical performance, the smoother the optimization.
- **Overly aggressive targets** (significantly above the category's typical ROAS) cause most pairs to fall below 50% of target, triggering progressive throttling across the campaign and potentially suppressing delivery.
- **Overly conservative targets** may underuse the optimizer, since most pairs will sit above target with little need for correction.

If you don't have a clear reference, start with a target close to your current ROAS in similar campaigns and adjust as you accumulate data.
